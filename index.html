Human-Inspired Adaptive Gait Learning for Humanoids Locomotion

Overview

Abstract

Achieving natural and efficient locomotion remains a central goal for humanoid control. While imitation learning enables humanoid robots to mimic human-like motions, the discrepancy in morphology and actuation between humans and robots often renders direct motion replication suboptimal. Moreover, ensuring robust locomotion under limited proprioceptive observations remains a critical challenge. This work proposes a human-inspired learning framework that integrates motion priors and historical state estimation to generate adaptive and energy-efficient gaits. The framework combines human-inspired task rewards with style rewards derived from retargeted human motion data to promote robust and human-like locomotion. To address partial observability, an adversarial learning structure is introduced, leveraging historical proprioceptive inputs and explicit base state estimation. A tri-metric evaluation protocol—covering gait symmetry, human-robot similarity, and energy efficiency—is established to assess human-likeness. Experimental results show that our approach produces highly natural and low-energy locomotion across varying conditions.
